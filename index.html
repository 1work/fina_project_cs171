COVID 19: THE INEQUALITY VIRUS

Who has been hit the hardest by the pandemic around the world, and why?

A Data Story by Minjue Wu, Workneh Jano, and Jonathan Zhang

COVID-19 has been the world's leading infectious killer, taking away over 6 million lives from its inception to the present day since the outbreak first started in 2019 in Wuhan, China. After the WHO declared it a global pandemic in March 2020, the whole world was thrown into disarray, but not all countries or populations suffered to the same extent. The lived experience of those impacted by COVID drastically varied from school to school, state to state, and country to country. Throughout the past two years, while many developed Western countries have seemingly returned to pre-pandemic "normalcy", there are communities who have and continue to face disproportionate adverse consequences. This is a story about how COVID-19 became the inequality virus, told in chronological order by its causes and impact: Past (GDP), Present (Incarceration Healthcare Access), and Future (Education Disruptions).


SECTION I: Ghosts from the Past

GDP and Vaccine Access Inequality Bar Nations from Preventative Care

For many countries, the unequal health outcomes from the pandemic came from poor resources and infrastructure that started long before the virus broke out. Not all countries had access to the same networks, finances, and supply chains to respond to COVID. Lower income countries in particular, left from decades of imperialist exploitation by Europe and the Americas, created great disparities in Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and lack of preventative resources like vaccines due to neoliberalism.

Take a look at the following scatterplot, which shows the relationships between nations' GDPs and the total number of COVID vaccines (initial shot boosters, etc.) obtained by their citizens. What do you notice?

It looks like GDP per capita positively correlates with vaccinations per 100 citizens in every region. This makes sense, since the higher resources you have per citizen, the better infrastructure you will have for purchasing larger quantities of vaccines and distributing them.

Let's now take a closer look at the distribution of the varieties of vaccines available to countries in each WHO region.

As you can see, the regions with the highest GDP per Capita, like the Eastern Mediterranean countries, the European countries, and the Americas also have some of the highest average number of vaccine types. By contrast, countries in Africa and the Western Pacific, where most of the vaccines are produced by human labor and factories, have both the lowest GDP per capita and the lowest access to the total number of vaccines and the types of vaccines available.

What does this mean for COVID inequality? Due to the vaccine being developed and primarily distributed to high-income countries, many low-income countries are left with little access to diverse vaccines. With supply chain difficulties, especially with the transportation and storage of vaccines, countries that are far away from ports and trade networks as well as those that are fewer resources have a much more difficult time procuring the vaccines. Lower GDP per capita countries thus need help with both financial support and infrastructure building to attain the same level of vaccination protection as their higher GDP counterparts.


Key Takeaway #1: Countries with lower GDPs don't benefit from the vaccines they produce. They need financial support to purchase and distribute higher quantity and types of vaccines.


SECTION II: Impacts on the Present

Incarceration Healthcare Access and Mobility Impairment

Since economic inequalities exacerbate COVID outcomes, what does it mean for vulnerable populations in a single country? Let's first take a look at the disparate outcomes in COVID survival and infection rates for one of the most underserved communities in the U.S.: the incarcerated.

Incarcerated individuals face an uphill battle as much of their rights are stripped away and have less access to good healthcare. Without great resources for health prospects, it makes it much more likely for those to not be vaccinated or to face higher mortality rates compared to the rest of the population as their overall health is subpar compared to control groups. SECTION III: Pandemic Effects into the Future

Education Disruption Across the World

As we consider the ways in which COVID has already disproportionately affected different populations, one of the longest lasting impacts we can expect is the worldwide disruptions to student learning. Obtaining a quality education is one of the most powerful sources of upward socioeconomic mobility, and lower education is linked to incarceration and lower lifetime earnings, resulting in a vicious cycle of lower resources, crime, inability to afford healthcare. The degree to which students have been affected is positively correlated with a country's development index: The most impacted students from the global south have attendance rates of less than half of all school-aged children.

Click through to see which countries under which categories have the worst attendance performance.

In the bar graph, one can see that the United States ranks relatively high in attendance rates compared to the rest of the world, but ranks middle of the pack compared to other developed nations. Those that are the most impacted by attendance rates from COVID are those in the African Continent and South East Asia.

Knowledge is power and many of the most impacted communities are those with the least amount of political influence and voice in society. Many lower-income countries do not have enough collective bargaining power. Students are at a vulnerable age where they cannot vote and advocate for themselves. Incarcerated individuals are often disenfranchised and many people are oblivious to their plight. It is only by understanding the severity of the situation and advocating for others can it is possible to enact top-down change and guarantee that everyone had a voice.


Key Takeaway #4: COVID disruptions most heavily impacted countries with the lowest development indices; these countries need additional educational support to catch their students up to the rest of the world.


RECOMMENDATIONS FOR THE FUTURE

While we see the majority of the United States and many of the developed countries in the world return to a semblance of normalcy, there are those who are still disproportionately impacted by the pandemic. Incarcerated persons, citizens of middle and low-income countries, and students in primary school are the most impacted in terms of survival rates, access to vaccines, and attendance in schools. These all intersect with one another and create a compounded negative effect that far outlasts the duration of the pandemic.

To address the inequities from the COVID-19 pandemic, we must address all three levels of causes and impact, past, present and future, to stop a cycle of vulnerable countries and populations from becoming even more harmed.

Firstly, we must focus on building infrastructures for buying and distributing vaccines in countries with low GDP.

Secondly, we must address health gaps for people with low mobility like incarcerated individuals to prevent them from being disproportionately harmed by lack of healthcare access.

Finally, we must share knowledge and educational sources across the global South in order to help countries develop their youth to fight the next pandemic.


About Us

We are Minjue Wu (Team leader), Workneh Jano, and Jonathan Zhang are three Harvard undergraduates. As a group, we have backgrounds in the History of Medicine, Social Studies, Political Science, Molecular and Cellular Biology, and Computer Science, and we explored how the COVID pandemic has been felt differently by populations around the world. We are especially interested in capturing the experiences of the most vulnerable and marginalized demographics, including children (disruptions in education), the incarcerated (healthcare provision in prison), and just in general how the pandemic has impacted mobility to resources like groceries or the pharmacy. Our goal is to highlight how COVID disproportionately impacts those who are the "Without (resources)", and those with low social mobility and/or socioeconomic status. We are motivated by how different we are pandemic experiences are compared to our family members in our native countries of China and Ethiopia, which all have differing levels of resources and infrastructures of care compared to our current base in the United States. We hope this project will help others analyze how the pandemic has impacted issues of equity and social justice from a healthcare or political science perspective.
